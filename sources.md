# Annotated Sources: Censorship or Legal Compliance?

This bibliography supports the proposed comparison of government requests to X for content removal and user information in the United States, Japan, India, Turkey, Germany, and Brazil. The period is July-December 2021 because it it the most recent X's archived reports that provide downloadable, country-level data for both request categories. Newer sources are included to explain changes in reporting and to provide background, not to be merged directly with the 2021 observations.

## 1. Core Data and Definitions

### X removal-request archive

**Use:** This should be the primary source for country-level legal removal demands, accounts specified, compliance rates, and actions such as withholding. Use the July-December 2021 CSV. The category can include demands from government entities and lawyers, so the team must check the requester definitions before calling every observation a government request.

Twitter, Inc. (2022, July 28). *Removal requests: July-December 2021*. X Transparency Center. [https://transparency.x.com/en/reports/removal-requests#2021-jul-dec](https://transparency.x.com/en/reports/removal-requests#2021-jul-dec)

### X information-request archive

**Use:** This should be the primary source for government requests for account information, accounts specified, and disclosure rates. The archive distinguishes routine, emergency, and preservation requests; the project should use the combined government-information category consistently across countries.

Twitter, Inc. (2022, July 28). *Information requests: July-December 2021*. X Transparency Center. [https://transparency.x.com/en/reports/information-requests#2021-jul-dec](https://transparency.x.com/en/reports/information-requests#2021-jul-dec)

### X guidelines for law enforcement

**Use:** This page defines the legal processes X says it requires for non-public account information, communication content, emergency disclosures, preservation, and content-removal requests. It is necessary for interpreting what an "information request" represents, but it describes X's policy rather than independently verifying its practice.

X Corp. (n.d.). *Guidelines for law enforcement*. X Help Center. Retrieved July 16, 2026, from [https://help.x.com/en/rules-and-policies/x-law-enforcement-support](https://help.x.com/en/rules-and-policies/x-law-enforcement-support)

### X country-withheld-content policy

**Use:** This explains jurisdiction-specific withholding, user notification, appeals, and the difference between withholding content locally and deleting it globally. It supports careful definitions in the report.

X Corp. (n.d.). *About country withheld content*. X Help Center. Retrieved July 16, 2026, from [https://help.x.com/en/rules-and-policies/post-withheld-by-country](https://help.x.com/en/rules-and-policies/post-withheld-by-country)

### X legal-request FAQ

**Use:** This provides X's description of notice, delayed notice, legal-request review, disclosure of account information, and exceptions. It is particularly useful for discussing what aggregate compliance rates omit.

X Corp. (n.d.). *Frequently asked questions about legal requests*. X Help Center. Retrieved July 16, 2026, from [https://help.x.com/en/rules-and-policies/x-legal-faqs](https://help.x.com/en/rules-and-policies/x-legal-faqs)

### X global reports for 2024

**Use:** These reports show how X's newer reporting format differs from the detailed 2021 archive. They should be used to document the loss of comparable country coverage, not combined with the 2021 country dataset. In 2024, only Japan and the EU appear as named units in both request tables.

X Corp. (2024). *Global transparency report: H1 2024* [Report]. [https://transparency.x.com/content/dam/transparency-twitter/2024/x-global-transparency-report-h1.pdf](https://transparency.x.com/content/dam/transparency-twitter/2024/x-global-transparency-report-h1.pdf)

X Corp. (2025). *Global transparency report: H2 2024* [Report]. [https://transparency.x.com/content/dam/transparency-twitter/2025/x-global-transparency-report_h2_2024.pdf](https://transparency.x.com/content/dam/transparency-twitter/2025/x-global-transparency-report_h2_2024.pdf)

## 2. Independent Records and Audits

### Lumen Database

**Use:** Lumen contains copies of takedown notices and legal demands voluntarily submitted by platforms, including Twitter. It can supply a small qualitative sample showing requester, legal rationale, target, and sometimes reported action. A notice's presence does not prove that it was valid or that X complied.

Lumen Database. (n.d.). *About Lumen*. Harvard Law School Library. Retrieved July 16, 2026, from [https://lumendatabase.org/pages/about](https://lumendatabase.org/pages/about)

### Rest of World investigation

**Use:** This is a model for turning Lumen records into a modest quantitative analysis. It compared Twitter's reported government orders before and after Musk's acquisition and openly discusses the limitations of platform-submitted Lumen data.

Brandom, R. (2023, April 27). Twitter is complying with more government demands under Elon Musk. *Rest of World*. [https://restofworld.org/2023/elon-musk-twitter-government-orders/](https://restofworld.org/2023/elon-musk-twitter-government-orders/)

### Rest of World replication dataset

**Use:** This downloadable dataset contains the records used in the Rest of World investigation. It can help the team understand workable fields and coding choices before manually coding its own small Lumen sample.

Rest of World. (2023). *Lumen-Rest of World project data* [Data set]. GitHub. [https://github.com/row-engineering/everything/blob/main/2023/twitter-lumen/lumen-rest-of-world-project-data.csv](https://github.com/row-engineering/everything/blob/main/2023/twitter-lumen/lumen-rest-of-world-project-data.csv)

### Ranking Digital Rights assessment of X

**Use:** This independent assessment evaluates X's disclosures about government surveillance and censorship demands. It is useful when discussing transparency gaps and the risks of treating company reports as complete records.

Ranking Digital Rights. (2025). *X: 2025 Big Tech Edition*. [https://rankingdigitalrights.org/bte25/companies/X](https://rankingdigitalrights.org/bte25/companies/X)

### Access Now Transparency Reporting Index

**Use:** This source defines good transparency reporting and gathers reports across technology companies. It helps the team evaluate whether X reports request types, legal authorities, outcomes, and user-data demands with enough detail.

Access Now. (n.d.). *Transparency Reporting Index*. Retrieved July 16, 2026, from [https://www.accessnow.org/campaign/transparency-reporting-index/](https://www.accessnow.org/campaign/transparency-reporting-index/)

## 3. Period-Matched Country Context

These reports cover June 2021-May 2022 and therefore align closely with the proposed July-December 2021 data. Use their narratives and "Limits on Content" and "Violations of User Rights" sections to interpret cases. Do not treat the scores as direct measures of X requests or use them to declare that one country is definitively more censorial than another.

### United States

**Use:** Provides context on strong constitutional speech protections alongside expansive law-enforcement and surveillance authorities. This helps explain why information-request volume and removal-request volume may differ.

Freedom House. (2022). *United States: Freedom on the Net 2022 country report*. [https://freedomhouse.org/country/united-states/freedom-net/2022](https://freedomhouse.org/country/united-states/freedom-net/2022)

### Japan

**Use:** Provides context for Japan's high internet-freedom score. It should be read alongside X's explanation that many Japanese removal demands concern financial crime, narcotics, or prostitution, which cautions against treating request volume alone as political censorship.

Freedom House. (2022). *Japan: Freedom on the Net 2022 country report*. [https://freedomhouse.org/country/japan/freedom-net/2022](https://freedomhouse.org/country/japan/freedom-net/2022)

### India

**Use:** Documents the 2021 Information Technology Rules, government blocking orders, platform obligations, and risks to online speakers. It is directly relevant to interpreting both content-removal and identity-disclosure requests.

Freedom House. (2022). *India: Freedom on the Net 2022 country report*. [https://freedomhouse.org/country/india/freedom-net/2022](https://freedomhouse.org/country/india/freedom-net/2022)

### Turkey

**Use:** Documents content-removal orders, platform-localization requirements, prosecutions over online speech, and surveillance concerns. It supports selecting Turkey as a contrasting high-removal case.

Freedom House. (2022). *Turkey: Freedom on the Net 2022 country report*. [https://freedomhouse.org/country/turkey/freedom-net/2022](https://freedomhouse.org/country/turkey/freedom-net/2022)

### Germany

**Use:** Explains Germany's relatively strong rights environment alongside the Network Enforcement Act and surveillance concerns. This makes Germany useful for distinguishing regulated content removal from an assumption of authoritarian censorship.

Freedom House. (2022). *Germany: Freedom on the Net 2022 country report*. [https://freedomhouse.org/country/germany/freedom-net/2022](https://freedomhouse.org/country/germany/freedom-net/2022)

### Brazil

**Use:** Explains the Marco Civil da Internet, court-ordered removal, intermediary liability, and privacy protections. It supplies legal context for Brazil's reliance on judicial orders directed at platforms.

Freedom House. (2022). *Brazil: Freedom on the Net 2022 country report*. [https://freedomhouse.org/country/brazil/freedom-net/2022](https://freedomhouse.org/country/brazil/freedom-net/2022)

## 4. Research Literature

### Censorship through fear and friction

**Use:** Roberts provides the project's main conceptual bridge from visible removal to less visible chilling effects. The project should borrow the concepts of fear and friction without assuming that evidence from China applies identically to all six countries.

Roberts, M. E. (2018). *Censored: Distraction and diversion inside China's Great Firewall*. Princeton University Press. [https://doi.org/10.23943/9781400890057](https://doi.org/10.23943/9781400890057)

### Platforms as governors of public speech

**Use:** Gillespie explains why platform decisions are governance decisions rather than purely technical actions. This supports analyzing X as an intermediary exercising discretion between governments and users.

Gillespie, T. (2018). *Custodians of the internet: Platforms, content moderation, and the hidden decisions that shape social media*. Yale University Press. [https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300235029/custodians-of-the-internet/](https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300235029/custodians-of-the-internet/)

### Platform governance framework

**Use:** Gorwa maps the relationships among platforms, governments, users, and other actors. It gives the final report a concise framework for explaining why "censorship" and "legal compliance" are not mutually exclusive labels.

Gorwa, R. (2019). What is platform governance? *Information, Communication & Society, 22*(6), 854-871. [https://doi.org/10.1080/1369118X.2019.1573914](https://doi.org/10.1080/1369118X.2019.1573914)

### Dataset of country-withheld tweets

**Use:** This study establishes that jurisdiction-specific withholding can be measured and documents a large historical sample of censored Twitter posts and accounts. Its dataset is much larger than this project needs, but its definitions and limitations are directly relevant.

Elmas, T., Overdorf, R., & Aberer, K. (2021). A dataset of state-censored tweets. *Proceedings of the International AAAI Conference on Web and Social Media, 15*(1), 1009-1015. [https://doi.org/10.1609/icwsm.v15i1.18124](https://doi.org/10.1609/icwsm.v15i1.18124)

### Effects of jurisdiction-specific withholding

**Use:** This newer study estimates how withholding affects engagement and follower growth. It supports the claim that local withholding can materially reduce a speaker's reach even when content remains available elsewhere.

Çetinkaya, Y. M., & Elmas, T. (2025). State and geopolitical censorship on Twitter (X): Detection and impact analysis of withheld content. *Proceedings of the 34th ACM International Conference on Information and Knowledge Management*. [https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.13375](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.13375)

### Limits of self-reported transparency data

**Use:** This study finds substantial inconsistencies in the EU Digital Services Act database and particularly in X's records. It supports careful language such as "X-reported compliance" rather than treating the figures as ground truth.

Trujillo, A., Fagni, T., & Cresci, S. (2025). The DSA Transparency Database: Auditing self-reported moderation actions by social media. *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction, 9*(2), 1-28. [https://doi.org/10.1145/3711085](https://doi.org/10.1145/3711085)

### Cross-platform DSA analysis

**Use:** This paper demonstrates that platforms use transparency categories differently. It provides methodological support for avoiding comparisons between metrics unless their definitions and denominators match.

Drolsbach, C. P., & Pröllochs, N. (2024). Content moderation on social media in the EU: Insights from the DSA Transparency Database. In *Companion proceedings of the ACM Web Conference 2024* (pp. 939-942). Association for Computing Machinery. [https://doi.org/10.1145/3589335.3651482](https://doi.org/10.1145/3589335.3651482)

### Automated transparency and platform discretion

**Use:** This paper explains why a standardized database can still permit substantial platform discretion and inconsistent disclosure. It is useful for the limitations and evaluation sections.

Kaushal, R., van de Kerkhof, J., Goanta, C., Spanakis, G., & Iamnitchi, A. (2024). Automated transparency: A legal and empirical analysis of the Digital Services Act Transparency Database. In *Proceedings of the 2024 ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency* (pp. 1121-1132). Association for Computing Machinery. [https://doi.org/10.1145/3630106.3658960](https://doi.org/10.1145/3630106.3658960)

## 5. Optional European Validation Source

### EU Digital Services Act Transparency Database

**Use:** The database can provide supplemental evidence about X moderation decisions in the EU. It should not be merged with the 2021 request dataset because it began in 2023 and measures individual moderation decisions rather than the same request-level units.

European Commission. (n.d.). *Digital Services Act Transparency Database*. Retrieved July 16, 2026, from [https://transparency.dsa.ec.europa.eu/](https://transparency.dsa.ec.europa.eu/)

## Recommended Minimum Source Set

For the project as currently scoped, the team only needs to analyze the following sources directly:

1. X's July-December 2021 removal-request CSV.
2. X's July-December 2021 information-request CSV.
3. X's legal-request definitions and country-withheld-content policy.
4. A small, documented sample of Lumen notices.
5. Freedom on the Net 2022 reports for the six countries.
6. Elmas et al. (2021), Gorwa (2019), and Ranking Digital Rights (2025) for interpretation and limitations.

The remaining sources are background or robustness material and should only be added if time permits.
